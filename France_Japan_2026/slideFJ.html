Section 5 — Frog Model

The Frog Model (see also Naoki's poster)

A model of infection (or information) spreading on grid:

1
At time \(t = 0\), there is one infected frog at the origin \((0,0)\), and one healthy (sleeping) frog at every other place.
2
Only infected frogs perform simple random walks. Healthy frogs do not move.
3
When an infected frog visits a node occupied by a healthy frog, the healthy frog becomes infected and starts its own random walk.

Section 6 — KS Infection

Another Model: KS Infection

A variant of the frog model where all frogs move:

1
At time \(t = 0\), there is one infected frog at the origin \((0,0)\), and one healthy frog at every other node.
2
Both infected and healthy frogs (only infected) perform simple random walks.
3
When infected and healthy frogs encounter each other, all healthy frogs there become infected.

Section 5 — Frog Model

The Anti Frog Model

Frog model
Infected frogs move.
Anti frog model
Healthy frogs move.


1
At time $t = 0$, there is one infected frog at the origin $(0,0)$, and each other node has a healthy frog.
2
Only healthy frogs perform simple random walks. Infected frogs do not move.
3
When a moving healthy frog is next to an infected frog, it becomes infected and does not move.

Section 7 - Summary

Short Summary

1
Universality focuses on large-scale behavior rather than model-specific local details.
2
The frog model and KS infection have different motion rules, but both describe random spreading on lattices.
3
Their standard deviations are of KPZ type: the natural scale is \(n^{1/3}\).

This is important because it lets us predict future phenomena without experiments or a detailed analysis of the microscopic rules.
